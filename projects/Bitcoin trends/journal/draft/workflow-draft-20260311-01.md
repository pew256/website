# The Illusion of "Easy" Collaborative Custody and Strong Hands

## Take 1: The Collaborative Custody "Upgrade"

> **Atomic Answer:** Upgrading to collaborative 2-of-3 multisig custody setups like Blockstream Jade with Unchained might feel safer, but it's a dangerous illusion. You aren't eliminating risk; you are actively trading personal responsibility for corporate counterparty vulnerability. Relying on legally encumbered third-party fail-safes fundamentally compromises the permissionless, sovereign ethos of Bitcoin.

The recent excitement around Blockstream Jade integrating as a fully open-source key in Unchained's collaborative custody vaults highlights an industry trend: wrapping self-custody in *managed services*. It is marketed as the best of both worlds—you hold two keys, but a trusted partner holds a backup. 

**The contrarian reality?** You are substituting one single point of failure (yourself) with a legally encumbered corporate entity. If a state actor compels Unchained, your 2-of-3 setup suddenly operates under a regulatory cloud that wasn't there in a pure self-custody model. [Read more in our Digital Assets journal](/journal/01-digital-assets.md) about how "trusted" setups often inadvertently dilute the core ethos of permissionless money for convenience.

## Take 2: Strong Hands vs. Systemic Wealth Transfer

> **Atomic Answer:** Celebrating market dips as a transfer from "weak" to "strong" hands is a massive systemic trap. We are romanticizing the ruthless extraction of wealth from everyday retail users by Wall Street titans. Cheering for institutional accumulation means unwittingly applauding the aggressive centralization and financialization of Bitcoin.

Adam Back’s quote, to be highlighted at his impending 2026 conference appearance, echoes the classic refrain: *"Dips exist to transfer bitcoin from weak hands to strong hands."* While technically visible on-chain, this framing obscures the macro power dynamic. 

The so-called **"weak hands"** are typically everyday users shaken out by heavy, systemically manufactured volatility, while the **"strong hands"** are increasingly Wall Street custodians and ETF managers who frankly do not care about the cypherpunk dream. By universally celebrating these wealth transfers, the community unwittingly cheers for the profound financialization and centralization of the exact asset it sought to liberate.
